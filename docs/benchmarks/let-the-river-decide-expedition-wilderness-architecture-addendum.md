# Let the River Decide — Expedition & Wilderness Architecture Addendum

## Governance notice

This addendum extends the unified Let the River Decide long-form multi-layer benchmark with expedition, wilderness, environmental, cultural-protocol, and witness-credibility calibration layers.

It does not replace the core benchmark. It adds specialty architecture for manuscripts where suspense emerges from journey, environment, distance, uncertainty, ecological absence, cultural knowledge, and leadership under pressure.

## Purpose

This benchmark extension teaches RevisionGrade how to evaluate narratives where danger does not require a conventional villain and where plot progression is carried by movement, environmental pressure, witness interpretation, and cumulative field evidence.

Core calibration rules:

1. Observation is not proof.
2. Absence may function as evidence.
3. Wilderness danger does not require a villain.
4. Competence builds reader trust.
5. Ethical withholding differs from narrative withholding.
6. Environment can function as antagonist.
7. Distance amplifies uncertainty.
8. Expedition narratives are movement-driven story systems.

---

## Layer 19 — Witness Credibility Architecture

Track the difference between what the narrator sees, believes, assumes, infers, and cannot know.

| Category | Required distinction | Evaluation obligation |
|---|---|---|
| Observed | Direct sensory or field evidence | Treat as testimony, not automatic objective truth. |
| Interpreted | Meaning assigned to observation | Test whether inference follows from evidence. |
| Assumed | Believed but not verified | Mark uncertainty rather than hard fact. |
| Unknown | Outside narrator knowledge | Preserve ambiguity when the text does. |
| Later confirmed | Observation validated by subsequent evidence | Track when credibility increases. |

Calibration rule:

> Witness testimony is not objective truth. A wilderness narrator may be honest, competent, and still limited.

Failure condition: an evaluation fails if it treats every field observation as omniscient fact or dismisses experiential knowledge as anecdote without textual cause.

## Layer 20 — Environmental Threat Architecture

Track threat sources that arise from setting, ecology, weather, remoteness, infrastructure failure, and human-caused environmental degradation.

| Threat type | Examples | Calibration rule |
|---|---|---|
| River hazard | current, cold, rapids, debris, drowning risk, navigation failure | Water movement can be plot pressure. |
| Weather hazard | cold, heat, storms, visibility, wind | Weather can function as antagonist-like pressure. |
| Wildlife hazard | absence, behavior change, predator risk, disease indicators | Wildlife matters both by presence and absence. |
| Terrain hazard | distance, isolation, portage difficulty, shoreline access | Geography can create suspense without villainy. |
| Human environmental hazard | contamination, abandoned infrastructure, industrial residue | Human damage can haunt the landscape. |
| Institutional hazard | missing response, poor records, jurisdictional gaps | Bureaucratic silence can be narrative pressure. |

Calibration rule:

> The river itself can function as antagonist, witness, archive, and pressure system.

## Layer 21 — Journey Structure Layer

Expedition narratives often progress through movement rather than conventional act turns. Required journey states:

1. Departure.
2. Commitment.
3. Escalation.
4. Adaptation.
5. Attrition.
6. Revelation.
7. Return or irreversible change.

| Journey phase | Function | Evaluation requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Departure | Establishes motive, preparation, route, and risk threshold | Identify why the journey begins and what promise it creates. |
| Commitment | Crossing point where easy exit disappears | Track when the journey becomes binding. |
| Escalation | Increasing environmental, physical, moral, or informational pressure | Do not require a villain for escalation. |
| Adaptation | Skills, routines, route decisions, risk management | Competence should be evaluated as craft evidence. |
| Attrition | Fatigue, uncertainty, loss, erosion of assumptions | Track cumulative cost. |
| Revelation | New understanding from field evidence | Revelation may be ecological or ethical, not only plot-twist based. |
| Return | Physical return or changed perception | Closure may be witness-based rather than conquest-based. |

Calibration rule:

> Movement itself may constitute plot progression.

## Layer 22 — Wilderness Suspense Layer

Wilderness suspense is not the same as thriller suspense. It depends on exposure, remoteness, uncertainty, and limited rescue options.

Required detection:

- Isolation.
- Exposure.
- Resource limitations.
- Distance from help.
- Navigation uncertainty.
- Communication limits.
- Escalating consequence of small decisions.

| Suspense driver | Reader effect | Calibration rule |
|---|---|---|
| Isolation | unease, vulnerability | Distance from others can be conflict. |
| Exposure | bodily anxiety | Physical vulnerability can replace chase mechanics. |
| Resource limits | practical dread | Scarcity creates stakes. |
| Uncertainty | interpretive tension | Not knowing can be the engine. |
| Rescue distance | consequence amplification | A small mistake can become major danger. |

Calibration rule:

> Danger does not require a villain.

## Layer 23 — Cultural Protocol Layer

This layer evaluates how the narrative handles Indigenous knowledge, local expertise, outsider assumptions, ethical boundaries, and respect protocols.

| Protocol domain | Evaluation requirement |
|---|---|
| Indigenous knowledge | Treat as knowledge system, not atmosphere or folklore. |
| Local expertise | Distinguish lived knowledge from official records. |
| Outsider assumptions | Track where narrator confidence may be limited. |
| Ethical boundaries | Respect that some information may be withheld appropriately. |
| Stewardship | Detect environmental care as action, not theme-only language. |

Calibration rule:

> Information can be withheld for ethical reasons, not merely narrative reasons.

Failure condition: an evaluation fails if it treats cultural knowledge only as exotic texture or demands extraction of knowledge the narrative ethically protects.

---

## Layer 24 — Evidence of Absence Layer

Expedition and ecological narratives often create meaning through what should be present but is missing.

| Missing element | Expected presence | Possible significance |
|---|---|---|
| Wildlife | species, tracks, calls, migration signs | ecological decline, disruption, fear, displacement |
| People | communities, travelers, witnesses, responders | abandonment, danger, silence, historical erasure |
| Infrastructure | maps, signs, safe routes, maintained access | institutional neglect or remoteness |
| Records | reports, official explanation, reliable history | contested truth or hidden harm |
| Sound | birdsong, animals, human activity | ecological silence or dread |

Calibration rule:

> Absence can function as evidence when the narrative establishes what should be there.

Failure condition: an evaluation fails if it ignores absence as a story signal or treats all missing information as authorial incompleteness.

---

## Layer 25 — Expedition Leadership Layer

Evaluate competence, not just personality.

| Leadership dimension | Required evaluation |
|---|---|
| Risk assessment | Does the leader recognize and weigh danger? |
| Decision quality | Are choices grounded in evidence, experience, and constraints? |
| Crisis response | Does pressure produce adaptation or collapse? |
| Team cohesion | Does leadership preserve trust? |
| Judgment under uncertainty | Does the narrative show how decisions are made without full information? |
| Humility | Does the leader adapt when field evidence contradicts assumption? |

Calibration rule:

> Competence generates reader trust.

Failure condition: an evaluation fails if it treats leadership only as bravery, dominance, or confidence.

## Reader Experience Progression

Expected progression:

```text
curiosity → exploration → unease → isolation → suspicion → ecological grief → revelation → changed understanding
```

The evaluator should track how physical movement changes reader knowledge and how field evidence transforms curiosity into moral or environmental pressure.

---

## Calibration Requirements

A compliant evaluation of Let the River Decide must identify:

- wilderness suspense quality;
- expedition structure quality;
- environmental threat architecture;
- witness credibility limits;
- evidence-of-absence signals;
- leadership competence;
- cultural protocol handling;
- ecological narrative integration;
- movement as plot;
- distance as suspense amplifier.

---

## Failure Conditions

An evaluation is incomplete if it:

- requires a conventional villain before recognizing suspense;
- ignores the river/environment as active pressure system;
- treats absence as missing authorial detail rather than possible evidence;
- treats field testimony as omniscient fact;
- ignores cultural knowledge protocols;
- misses leadership/decision quality as reader-trust architecture;
- treats expedition movement as travel filler rather than plot.
