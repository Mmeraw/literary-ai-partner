# 13 Story Evaluation Criteria (v1.0.0)

**Canonical Version:** 1.0.0  
**Binding Authority:** WAVE Revision Guide  
**Last Updated:** February 21, 2026  
**Status:** Immutable (frozen for releases)

## OVERVIEW

These 13 criteria form the complete rubric for evaluating narrative manuscripts. They assess narrative quality, coherence, and professional readiness without prescribing style or voice.

**CRITICAL:** These criteria are derived from and servant to the WAVE Revision Guide (see `docs/WAVE_REVISION_GUIDE_CANON.md`). If a criterion conflicts with WAVE canon, WAVE canon governs.

---

## THE 13 CRITERIA

### 1. **Concept & Core Premise**
**What it measures:** Is the central idea compelling, original, and clearly legible? Does the premise generate inherent narrative tension?

**Why it matters:** A strong concept is the foundation. Weak concepts cannot be rescued by execution alone. Agents read for originality and the promise of sustained tension.

**Red flags:**
- Premise is derivative or unclear
- Central tension is not apparent by page 10
- Concept doesn't sustain a full manuscript

---

### 2. **Narrative Drive & Momentum**
**What it measures:** Does the story maintain forward movement through escalation, consequence, and evolving stakes?

**Why it matters:** Momentum is the reader's experience of pressure. Without it, pages drag. With it, readers turn them fast.

**Red flags:**
- Same conflict repeats without escalation
- Stakes don't raise after page 50
- Scenes feel disconnected or meandering

---

### 3. **Character Depth & Psychological Coherence**
**What it measures:** Are characters internally consistent, motivated, and capable of meaningful change or resistance to change?

**Why it matters:** Characters must feel real—which means contradictions are okay if they're human, not convenient.

**Red flags:**
- Character acts against established motivation without reason
- No sense of internal conflict or stakes within the character
- Motivations feel author-driven rather than character-driven

---

### 4. **Point of View & Voice Control**
**What it measures:** Is the narrative perspective stable, intentional, and appropriate to the material?

**Why it matters:** POV is the reader's access point. Unstable POV breaks immersion instantly.

**Red flags:**
- POV slips between characters mid-scene
- Narrative distance shifts without intention
- Voice is inconsistent (character sounds different in different scenes)

---

### 5. **Scene Construction & Function**
**What it measures:** Does each scene perform a narrative function (reveal, escalate, complicate, or resolve)?

**Why it matters:** Every scene should earn its space. Filler is immediately apparent to agents.

**Red flags:**
- Two scenes accomplish the same thing
- Scene ends with no change in power, knowledge, or stakes
- Pacing is unclear (unclear what the scene is for)

---

### 6. **Dialogue Authenticity & Subtext**
**What it measures:** Does dialogue reveal character and power dynamics without unnecessary exposition?

**Why it matters:** Good dialogue shows who people are. Bad dialogue explains things the scene already showed.

**Red flags:**
- Dialogue is expository ("As you know, I'm your brother")
- No subtext—characters say what they mean directly
- Dialogue all sounds the same (no character voice distinction)

---

### 7. **Thematic Integration**
**What it measures:** Are themes embedded through action and consequence rather than stated directly?

**Why it matters:** Agents hate declared themes. Themes must emerge through what happens, not what's said about what happens.

**Red flags:**
- Narrator explains what the scene "means"
- Explicit moral lesson at scene end
- Theme is safe or predictable

---

### 8. **World-Building & Environmental Logic**
**What it measures:** Are physical spaces, social systems, and rules internally consistent and credible?

**Why it matters:** Credibility is earned through consistency, not description. Readers must trust the world.

**Red flags:**
- System behavior is vague or changes without explanation
- Geography is unclear or contradicts itself
- Rules are broken without consequence (or consequence is unclear)

---

### 9. **Pacing & Structural Balance**
**What it measures:** Is the rhythm of tension and release effective across the work as a whole?

**Why it matters:** Pacing is not just speed—it's the architecture of pressure and recovery. One without the other is exhausting.

**Red flags:**
- First 50 pages draggy, then relentless action
- No recovery beats; reader never breathes
- No escalation; repeated scenes of same intensity

---

### 10. **Prose Control & Line-Level Craft**
**What it measures:** Is the prose precise, intentional, and free from unmotivated repetition or ambiguity?

**Why it matters:** Agents read fast and spot sloppy prose instantly. Precision signals professionalism.

**Red flags:**
- Repetition of words, phrases, or images (accidental)
- Clichés or AI-adjacent language (heartpounding, airtight)
- Overuse of filter verbs, adverbs, or body-part tells

---

### 11. **Tonal Authority & Consistency**
**What it measures:** Does the work maintain tonal integrity without unintended shifts or register drift?

**Why it matters:** Tone is the voice's embodiment. Inconsistent tone signals lack of control.

**Red flags:**
- Suddenly shifts to a different register mid-chapter
- Humor appears in a serious scene without setup
- Emotional weight is inconsistent (same event treated as minor, then major)

---

### 12. **Narrative Closure & Promises Kept**
**What it measures:** Are narrative promises fulfilled, intentionally subverted, or explicitly left open? Does the work avoid unresolved threads unless deliberately framed as such?

**Why it matters:** Readers expect implicit contracts to be honored. Unresolved threads feel like abandoned work, not intentional ambiguity.

**Red flags:**
- Plot thread is introduced then forgotten
- Character arc is incomplete
- Ending feels unearned or tacked-on

---

### 13. **Professional Readiness & Market Positioning**
**What it measures:** Does the manuscript demonstrate control, cohesion, and clarity consistent with professional publication standards?

**Why it matters:** This is the "agent lens" criterion. A manuscript at 9 or 10 on the others but 5 on this one signals "not ready."

**Red flags:**
- Lack of copy-editing (typos, grammar)
- Structural issues that suggest rushed drafting
- Tone/voice suggests amateur (over-explanation, summary language, telling instead of showing)

---

## HOW THESE CRITERIA RELATE TO WAVE

Each criterion is grounded in WAVE diagnostic passes:

- **#1, #2, #9:** Early WAVES (structural truth, momentum)
- **#3, #4, #7:** Mid WAVES (character coherence, voice, theme)
- **#5, #6, #8:** Mid WAVES (scene function, dialogue, world logic)
- **#10, #11:** Late WAVES (prose control, tonal authority)
- **#12, #13:** Closure and professional polish (late WAVES)

All criteria are **evaluable without prescribing style**. A manuscript can be evaluative any criterion and still retain unique voice.

---

## SCORING FRAMEWORK (Informational Only)

Each criterion can be scored 1–10:

- **9–10:** Mastery / no notes
- **7–8:** Professional / minor tweaks
- **5–6:** Competent / needs work
- **3–4:** Weak / major revision needed
- **1–2:** Critical failure / starting over

**For publication readiness, all 13 must be 7+. Anything below 7 is a gate closure.**

## IMPLEMENTATION RULES (Binding on Platforms)

1. **Completeness:** All 13 criteria must be evaluated in every assessment.
2. **Authority:** WAVE canon supersedes platform defaults or model inference.
3. **Transparency:** Evaluation results must include explicit scores or assessments for all 13 criteria.
4. **Immutability:** These criteria cannot be modified, renamed, or reordered by platforms. Version control is the only authority.
5. **Audit Trail:** Any divergence from these 13 must be documented and justified against WAVE canon.
