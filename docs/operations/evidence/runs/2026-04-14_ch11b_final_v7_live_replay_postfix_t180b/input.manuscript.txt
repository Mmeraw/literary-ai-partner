Chapter 11b – Mayan Train Approval (FINAL) (V7)
Location: Gold Bridge Library
Date: 23 July 2025
The electric heater popped in the corner, a sharp metallic tick that seemed to echo longer than it should have. The air smelled faintly of baked dust, cedar, and paper that had been sitting too long in the dark.
On the table between us, a big map of British Columbia lay sprawled and patient under Cliff’s elbows. His coffee cup left a damp ring in the Skeena watershed. A pencil sat beside the fresh hunting regulations printout he’d coaxed from the library’s sputtering copier — edges still warm.
He traced arcs north toward goat country, east toward Plunkett, Saskatchewan, his lines lazy as weather fronts. The pencil paused over the Chilcotin Plateau before drifting again.
I was still buzzing from the afternoon’s search — the Gold Bridge Library’s weak Wi-Fi, its scratchy keyboards, the clunk of the old printer, the smell of toner. I’d gone in chasing the Mackenzie Dam’s paper trail: land leases, environmental review schedules, RCMP security contracts. Instead, I’d come out with something else entirely. Not a detour. A mirror.
“The Yucatán,” I said.
Cliff didn’t look up.
“They’re cutting a rail line through the jungle,” I said. “Fifteen hundred kilometers. Across limestone that’s basically a sponge. All the water lives underground — cenotes, rivers so clear you can see the bones of fish on the bottom. The Maya call them sacred.”
He kept tracing.
“They’ve got the National Guard posted at the job sites. Rifles. Environmental reviews — rushed or skipped. Half the villages never even knew the consultations were happening, but the reports tick the box. Promises of jobs that vanish the day the last rail spike’s in. Promises of tourism, but the hotels go to outsiders.”
Cliff grunted. “Sounds familiar.”
“It should. Swap limestone for permafrost and glacier-fed rivers. Swap cenotes for salmon beds. Swap jaguar corridors for caribou migration routes. Swap the National Guard for RCMP and contractors in branded jackets. The Mackenzie Dam will do to Robert’s people exactly what the Mayan Train is doing to the Maya — bulldoze the fishing camps, drown the bends that hold the stories. And in the final report, it’ll all be filed under ‘mitigation measures.’”
His pencil stopped mid-arc, the tip resting on the map. “Same playbook?”
“Same pattern. Everywhere.”
I turned my laptop toward him, showing headlines cached before the Wi-Fi could choke. Grainy photographs: railbeds carved into green, protest camps sagging under tarps, soldiers in fatigues leaning on rifles at chain-link fences.
I scrolled to the section I’d saved.
________________________________________
Excerpt – Secretaría de Medio Ambiente y Recursos Naturales (SEMARNAT)
Mayan Train – Environmental Impact Summary (Section 4.2)
“Certain karstic formations (cenotes, subterranean rivers) will be intersected by the construction corridor. Where direct alteration occurs, mitigation shall consist of: a) technical stabilization of exposed limestone, b) relocation of aquatic fauna where feasible, c) compensatory reforestation within the municipality of impact.
Community consultations have been conducted in all relevant administrative zones, with meeting attendance noted in official registers. Future monitoring will be coordinated with local representatives appointed through the municipal offices.
Estimated permanent alteration of groundwater flow: ≤ 12%.
This is within acceptable national standards for infrastructure projects of this scope.”
________________________________________
Cliff leaned forward, squinting as if proximity could change the words. “‘Acceptable national standards,’” he muttered. “For killing what’s sacred.”
“It’s not new,” I said. “Go back centuries. Colonial period — hacienda owners and Spanish encomenderos manipulating Indigenous leaders into signing away communal land. Sometimes under duress, sometimes for silver, livestock, political favor. Almost never honored. Once the ink dried? They paid rent to work the same fields they’d owned for generations. Water was the real choke point — no well without the owner’s permission.”
He didn’t interrupt.
“That pattern didn’t die with the Spaniards. Under Porfirio Díaz, late 1800s into the early 1900s, vast tracts were privatized for agriculture, mining, railroads. Indigenous land swallowed whole. Rail lines laid in the name of modernization. The aquifers, the grazing lands, the sacred places — all gone under a banner of progress. Same script. New uniforms.”
I switched windows on the laptop, pulled up the file I’d clipped from Ottawa before the library’s connection timed out.
________________________________________
Excerpt – Impact Assessment Agency of Canada
Mackenzie Hydroelectric Development – Draft Environmental Summary (Section 5.1)
“Impacts to salmon spawning grounds in designated riparian zones are projected to be moderate to high. Mitigation shall include: a) engineered spawning channels in suitable offset locations, b) provision of community employment during construction phase, c) cultural interpretation signage in visitor centers.
Engagement with Indigenous stakeholders has been initiated, with ongoing meetings scheduled. Agreement in principle is expected prior to final water license approval.
Project will provide clean, renewable energy for an estimated 1.2 million households.”
________________________________________
He read it slowly, then looked up. “They’re reading from the same script.”
“Exactly. Doesn’t matter if it’s limestone aquifers in the Yucatán or glacier-fed rivers in the Northwest Territories — the language is the same. The mitigation’s the same. The result’s the same. Robert’s people lose their salmon, their river, and in twenty years they’re tenants on the banks they used to own.”
From across the room, one of the knitting women laughed, the sound small and sharp against the hush. Her needles clicked in quick syncopation, like insect wings in the rafters. Outside, snow thickened under the orange cone of the streetlamp. The heater pinged again, as if marking time we didn’t have.
Cliff leaned back, tapping the eraser of his pencil against the Mackenzie drainage basin. “So our cenotes are salmon beds. Our jaguars are caribou. Our National Guard is the RCMP. And Ottawa’s the hacienda owner.”
“Exactly. Doesn’t matter the century. Doesn’t matter the latitude. The people on the land pay the highest price.”
He studied the basin a moment longer. “And you think this proves the river decides?”
“I think it proves the river remembers,” I said. “And it’s not in the habit of forgiving.”
