The men weren’t alone. Aqua World was full of life, but they mostly ignored it, partly because they couldn’t see or hear it and somewhat because it didn’t interest them. They were high, living more inside their heads than out.
A group of juvenile Columbian spotted frogs was studying them, though. They were watching from behind the lakeside grasses and rushes, far enough away that they could tolerate the giants’ smell. True to form, the black-haired, furless creatures took the clearing closest to the shoreline. They avoided the locations nearest the forest’s edge, likely because of the mosquitoes. The frogs wondered why the taller of the two, a pale-skin with red spots on his face, was calling out? No others were around except a skinnier sandy brown coloured one, and he rarely seemed to listen.
When frogs called out, others joined in. Sandy had gone to his sleeping area, completely ignoring his companion. The frogs didn’t see any female giants, and no other males were challenging the territory, so why was Red-spots vocalizing so loudly? Over and over, he was repeating something rhythmical with a buildup and a dying down; it sounded comforting. 
While bellowing, Red-spots was making fire and inhaling smoke. Giants had tremendous influence with Fire Gorf (one of the Columbian spotted frog’s gods). She’d shown them how to harness fire to keep warm, frighten off delicious insects, and burn animal flesh. They’d set the woods ablaze when they were feeling silly or annoyed. When they intentionally inhaled smoke, it either calmed or agitated them. The fumes that smelled like bittersweet tree-ash created the most excitation. When spastic, they’d run through the forest or jump into the lake naked, mounting one another like frogs.
They’d also cajoled Water Gorf to give them silly water, which they drank from their urns. They’d stumble around and pass out when they drank too much. Their carelessness was odd since survival in Aqua World depended on being alert.
	Red-spots continued to sing. Maniacal laughter erupted, and he shifted the highs and lows in his vocalization while blowing clouds of bittersweet smoke. Now cock-eyed, his walleyed eyepoppers threatened to decouple from his body. He stood up and started jumping around like a cricket.

Normally it would’ve been chilly-vanilly time for the Generation Zers, but Brutus seemed possessed and was re-energized with some deep, dark jungle beat in his head. His head movement took up a staccato rhythm, and his hands took to the air to pound some imaginary drums. Not one to lose his cadence, he sang out in a baritone voice, “Let’s . . . go . . . search . . . for . . . some . . . big . . . frogs . . . and . . . see . . . what’s . . . inside . . . their . . . heads!”
	Back to a more regular rhythm, but with his head still bobbing like a pigeon walkin’ along struttin’ its stuff, he upped the frequency of his speech. “Maybe we’ll find a stone. Then we can try eatin’ the legs!”
	Chey responded, “Talk about killin’ two birds with one stone. Get it?”
	“Lame. Yeah, yeah, yeah, I got it. Hey! They say frogs taste like chicken. Let’s boil some up and find out!”
	“Gross. How about I stick to my meat lover’s pizza instead.”
	“We could catch a couple and bring ’em home as pets!”
	“Nah, too much trouble. What would we feed ’em? Crickets? Where would we get those? We don’t know anythin’ about frogs.”
Chey wasn’t as rabid in his determination to harm innocents. Besides, his forever friend Mary Jane, inhaled only moments ago, encouraged him to relax and soak in the environment. He did her bidding and cast his eyes to the world around him.
The lake’s outer ring revealed what must’ve been or still were major inner struggles for life. Jagged mountains reached for the sky as if wanting to escape, but balls and chains tethered them. They’d thrown a temper tantrum, as evidenced by the rockslides. 
He thought, Mother Earth’s a chameleon, and her persona reflects her mood. Ever the egotist, she’s continually attempting to win over new admirers even as she crushes fervent followers. She’ll experiment with new things to curry favour, including new garb, pretending she’s somehow hip and cool. Then she’ll command her universal army to preen, primp and groom her, filling in holes, smoothing over crevices, giving her a bigger bust and fatter waist. On the surface, she appears to be open to new designs, colours, fabrics, textures, and the like since extended periods pass with relative calm. But she eventually tires with the proposed fashion of the day. Maybe that contributes to her moodiness, or perhaps she’s struggling with arthritis or the ageing process. She grumbles about this or that; flexes her big muscular tectonic slabs as if she’s in some Gold’s Gym mirror doing a look at me movement; disrobes; tosses aside her gym clothes and exposes unsightly new and old scars, scabs, and pimples. No wonder she’s bitter. When she misbehaves like this, some give her the benefit of the doubt, citing how she’s a staid, conservative, old-fashioned girl just wanting to return to her roots: out with the new, in with the old. To others like Brutus, she’s just a plain, old bitch, a chronic complainer who wields too much power, forcing earthlings to bow before her and get on their knees and submit. If one didn’t comply, she’d enact punishment. A favourite of hers is to put away her purse and take out the mass-extinction hammer from her toolkit. It’s the quickest way for her to deal with naysayers, non-conformists, and miscreants.
While Chey contemplated life, hoping time would stand still, his best friend made his way up to the opposite side of the hill overlooking the lake to perform a life-giving, alchemical ritual in a dark recess of the earth.

Several months earlier, the Columbian spotted frogs had been preparing to hibernate. The commoners were first to bury themselves, followed by council members, sentinels, and the crown.
One of the council members’ final duties was to check the site’s security to verify that a casual swimmer-by wouldn’t notice them. They were also to secretly do the bidding of the head of state, maiming or killing troublemakers, especially non-believers of Gorf. Murder was contrary to the Divine Will, but Crown Hyla rationalized it. With frogs in a sleep state, killing was easy to do. They’d stab gang leaders, so followers would see the pierced corpses when the thaw came, providing the intended message: obey or else. When they needed to destroy evidence and erase the memory of a frog, an appointed executioner would dislodge the slumbering victim from the lakebed and free-float the malefactor towards the abyss. Silver-scale fish would dispense of these heinous ones—no honourable burial or grave marker for them.
	Maiming frogs was a punishment for those who deserved to die but for which rapid death was considered too kind. Awakening with one or two missing eyes or limbs was much more interesting to those in power. They’d watch with delight as the mutilated tried to navigate the world not wholly intact post-thaw. They’d have side bets on how long the pathetic creatures could survive.
	The council members’ hibernation chant was neither about the nation nor belonging and love. The hymn was in praise of Gorf, requesting She provide the strength to get through the next ice age and wisdom to lead beyond that. Once the aristocratic elders went under, the sentinels followed shortly after that.
	Only the monarch slept alone; the others paired up. If a third frog was left out, it was permitted to join any non-council member twosome.
	Before slipping under the mud, Crown Hyla sought out her son, Zimeon. Gorf had commanded her to do a transference with him; he was the Chosen One. Her foremothers had taught power and wisdom were not only learned but also transferred by the blood of royalty. It wouldn’t take hold, though, unless compatibility existed. She also hoped to dilute the savage blood of his Orgon father.
	She’d tried a transference once before, but it’d led to death. Leviana never emerged from her suspended state. It seemed as if some creature had drained her bodily fluids as she slept. Hoping to immortalize her, Hyla had participated in burying the corpse, being sure not to mention her role as the likely killer. As the keeper of the toadstone, she knew she was privileged and could take individual liberties. As she helped move muck with her hind feet to bury the criminal evidence forever, she, who’d yet again gotten away with it, chanted the Divine Will of Gorf passed on by the rancients.
	
	“Allow Gorf to shape our destiny
	Preserve our perpetuity
	Protect the keeper of the toadstone
	Obey the keeper of the toadstone
	Colony before self
	Love, nurture, and protect one another”
	
	Overseeing the nation required specific dogma to be emphasized and encouraged. The supreme leader had to lead by example; shortcomings displays could lead to self-doubt by the population. Commoners acknowledged Preserve our perpetuity meant, in part, frogs aren’t to kill one another. Such a transgression would lead to banishment from the colony for one complete moon cycle. Such time away was as painful as a wasp sting since colony members suffered when away from the community.
	Now here was Hyla again, attempting to carry out Gorf’s will. She bit into her little one’s back and tasted his blood to see if it differed from Leviana’s. It didn’t, and it had a similar taste to the reddish-brown water leaching into the lake from an aqueduct to the east. She bit into the webbing of her left foot and rubbed her mucous and blood into him. She did this for a time, ensuring their binding would be complete. She applied pressure and waited for the clotting to take place. It didn’t take long for the bottom-feeding cranefly and midge larvae to circle and lap up the crimson, still-oozing nutrients. Where there’s blood, there’s life, she thought. Not one to pass up a meal, she gulped up the larvae, adding to her already full belly.
	Having done Gorf’s bidding, it was her turn to join the rest of the colony. But first, she had to do something personal. Like the coun¬cil members, the crown played Gorf in Aqua World. But she alone decided which council members would live or not. Such an implied threat saw the fiercest of loyalties towards her. No colony member could maim or kill her while she slumbered since the keeper of the toadstone was always the last to self-bury and the first to wake up. As the Crown of the Columbia spotted frogs, she was the Appointed One, and she and the others believed that Gorf had extended her this divine sleeping privilege. (Their beliefs were false, though; something else gave her this unique ability.)
	She considered the loyalty of her council. Arcana had been spreading doubt about the power of their Supreme Being, insisting each species had its Almighty, and in the sky above, they were fighting each other, and theirs must be losing. (Why else was there such deep suffering?) The enforcer decided not to maim or kill the Philosopher but instead inflict a wound that would carry permanent pain, reminding her not to be so sinful. Previous verbal admonition hadn’t worked, hence the escalation. 
	Hyla swam to the buried naysayer, webbed back the mud over her left eye, and pierced the outer flesh with one of her teeth ever so slightly. There was minimal bleeding from the eye, so death wasn’t a concern. Upon awakening, she’d know she’d been abused, forcing her to reflect more deeply on her comments and actions in the future, perhaps even seeking out royal one-on-one counsel and forgiveness. If sought out, the queen would provide a little-known plant with curing properties to accelerate the healing process. But first, the blasphemer would have to repent. The disciplinarian re-covered the offender. 
	For hibernation, colony members formed concentric circles, with the oldest members near the centre and the youngest on the outside. The crown’s place was in the centre. Council members didn’t face each other in pairs but instead created a frog link by holding one another’s front toepads, then moved to create a large circle facing inward towards the monarch. Should none emerge from sleep, the positioning would help any sister colony identify the one who was deserving of a royal burial.
	Once the ice age passed, the crown would be the first to rise. She was to assess the environment before others arose and, if necessary, invoke the power of the amulet to ward off any danger. If unable, she’d rouse her sentinels, who were to lure any beast of prey away, even if that required self-sacrifice.
	Where it concerned the toadstone, the Columbians had many beliefs: if the keeper prayed intensely enough, wisdom would come, and in some cases, miracles would flow; the longer the keeper didn’t use it, the more power it conferred; outside the body, the amulet would provide a more direct link to Gorf. However, coughing it up could sometimes lead to death due to internal bleeding and infection. Things were meant to go down and not come up, and the object was sizable and hard, not soft like some regurgitated insect matter.
	The sovereign was under constant pressure to engage in self-sacrifice and bring it up. The youth rebelled when there was grave hardship within the colony, and the crown wouldn’t use it. She professed to believe there were no shortcuts in life and insisted colony members work out their problems without using the talisman.
	Finally, all in order, Hyla took up her position in the middle of the concentric rings and buried herself. With the council members’ hibernation chant in her head, she hummed herself to sleep.

	Sun Gorf had worked Her magic. The snow and ice from the lake had melted once again, and creatures were abuzz with life. 
	Within the Columbian spotted frog colony, Zimeon was the first to emerge from the deep sleep. Being half Columbian and Orgon explained his early rising. He didn’t know it yet, but given their warmer climate, the Orgon frog species rarely hibernated, and when they did, it was for shorter periods.
	Curious and alone, he pondered the whisperings of the seniors. Soon he’d be a minor, perhaps selected to leave the colony to look for the New World. He didn’t want to be like the mayflies he’d seen, there one moment, gone the next. He was eager to become an elder since they could embrace their inner and outer blemishes without judgment. There seemed to be only one alternative to collective longevity. Yet not one inhabitant spoke of it, and that was to venture east towards the Dead Zone: a place rumoured to be the cause of mass death and where the ingredients for life (air, water, soil, and food) were grossly compromised. Even as our future could be in that direction, Crown Hyla never sends scouts that way. I’ll have to ask her about that, he thought.
	Going there was forbidden. Rumour had it those who’d gone had never returned, leaving a frog bone graveyard piled as high as the tallest reeds. The scents from the east winds gave no clue about the life forces in the distance. He estimated it’d take him two sunrises to swim there.
	Leaving the safety of the lake, Zimeon climbed a tree to see farther out. Exiting the water for extended periods was risky because his semi-permeable skin made him susceptible to dehydration, and if his skin didn’t remain moist, he’d suffocate. The elders told him to keep his slimy, mucous membrane wet, and Cloud Gorf encouraged him by providing fresh rain.
	With his webbed feet acting like suction cups, he used his sturdy hind legs to move upward. His only concern was falling. He found his legs shaking at one point, and he wasn’t sure if it was due to fear, fatigue, or hunger. He stopped rather than risk imminent death. In the distance, the low-morning light illuminated an alcove leading to an eastward-facing valley. The cliffs seemed devoid of soil. There were few large trees perched atop, and what flora was there clung to the rock made possible by their talon-like roots piercing into the stone’s flesh.
           Along the faraway shoreline, there were scattered albino logs, bleached by Sun Gorf or something sinister and unknown, Zimeon thought. There was one measure of order to the north—a beaver dam; from its size, he estimated it must’ve been there for many frog generations. It appeared there was life in the Dead Zone, after all. Most importantly, tall rushes were in the water, and there seemed to be a waterway flowing away from the lake and into a valley. Could a stream or marsh be there, a genesis, instead of the decay in Aqua World? Zimeon wanted to find out. 
	He made his way down and feasted on algae and snails to regain strength. Once rested, he swam back to inspect the others. There was still no movement from the hidden network of frogs, and he wondered why only he’d emerged.

	About the same Zimeon was stirring, so too was his father, Lord Thorander. His first duty was to verify the perimeter security of the pond he’d been hiding in. 
	Once done, he made his way to the distant, valley-floor, stopover area where he’d first arrived over two ice ages ago. He looked for telltale signs of the tundra swan sky-movers that had flown him to Aqua World. He wanted to return to his birthplace far to the south and needed them to take him there.
	With Sun Gorf towering overhead, the snow-capped mountains shimmered, winking a silent good-bye to the azure-blue sediment in the glacier flows. Thorander wondered if they were the guest types that never wanted to leave when the great thaw came or the kind that raced away, not even saying “thank you” to their majestic hosts for their long-term stay? The low-land plain had been carved out by those running away, although now it seemed the relics of those remaining were aged, sentimental fools since they were not fleet of foot and barely burbled preferring calm and peace over rolling-roaring-good times. The river meandered as dilly-dalliers, who were clinging to recent memories, took sweeping turns to peer over their shoulders. It was as if they were trying to go back from where they came. Perhaps they were homesick and wanted to see remaining loved ones.
	Fringes of dense, tall green and yellow grasses and shrubs dotted the surface. In the wind, they looked like bristling fur. To Thorander’s disappointment, he heard no honking and didn’t find any feces or grasses clipped short by the beaks of the migratory creatures.
	He felt betrayed by Gorf, so rather than pray thanks for a new awakening, he shook his fists at the sky and yelled, “What do You want from me? I’d have come back here after the ice age passed. Why’d You put my life at risk and force me to hibernate? I may not have awakened.” 
	The lively, dream sediment he saw coursing in the river became torturous, rage powder. He envisioned an evil, destructive force somewhere upstream, pulverizing the rock, making it submit, then kicking the particles into one of Water Gorf’s aqua-fears where they’d be left to drown.
	On his way back to the pond, his anger subsided, and his thoughts became more rational, Gorf must’ve had a reason for changing the migratory pattern of the sky-movers. Perhaps it was too dangerous for me to return, especially with Rana. Could it be that the Almighty is being overly cautious with our lives? Does He have a purpose for us? Is it that He wants us to integrate into Aqua World? Until we eradicate the blight, I can’t bring my daughter to the central state to meet the Columbians. Gorf brought Hyla and me together for a reason and gave us welcome additions from our coupling. Although ignorant and inferior, the Columbia spotted frogs are a kind, loving species. Perhaps my role is to help transition them through these difficult times by nursing them back to health and teaching them how to counter the various threats. 
	Jerking his head skyward, Thorander began bargaining with Gorf. “You win. I’ll do Your bidding and help the Columbians. But I don’t want to live out my life being an advisor and teacher. I wish to command and procreate so heavily that my lineage saturates the world. If You don’t immortalize me, my progeny will. And I need an amulet to do so—give me my toadstone!” To solidify his deal, Thorander squatted, splayed all four legs out, touched his head to the ground, closed his eyes, and chanted the Patriarch’s Divine Will of Gorf.
