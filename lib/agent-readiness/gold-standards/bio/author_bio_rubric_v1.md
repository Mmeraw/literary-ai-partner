# Author Bio Rubric v1

## Purpose

This rubric evaluates Agent Readiness author biographies for query letters, standalone submission packages, QueryTracker-style forms, and website/press use.

## Core test

A professional author bio must answer:

```txt
Why is this author credible to write this manuscript, in this category, for this audience?
```

## Scoring pillars

### 1. Fact governance

**Pass**

- Uses only author-supplied facts.
- Does not invent credentials, awards, publications, residences, platforms, education, or identity claims.
- Distinguishes verified facts from optional author-supplied positioning.

**Fail**

- Adds credentials not supplied.
- Implies publication history or recognition not present.
- Claims residence, identity, education, or professional expertise not supplied.

### 2. Manuscript relevance

**Pass**

- Selects facts that explain why this author is credible for this manuscript.
- Connects lived experience, professional experience, region, theme, or subject matter to the book.

**Fail**

- Lists credentials without connecting them to the manuscript.
- Uses generic lines such as "has always loved stories" or "hopes readers connect."

### 3. Professional compression

**Pass**

- Fits the target bio type.
- Query bio: usually 75-130 words.
- QueryTracker/short form: usually 50-85 words.
- Agent submission bio: usually 120-190 words.
- Website/press bio: may be longer and more branded.

**Fail**

- Query bio becomes a résumé.
- Website-style branding is inserted into a tight query letter.
- Bio overwhelms the story pitch.

### 4. Authority without overstatement

**Pass**

- Uses strong authority facts confidently.
- Avoids hype, defensive explanation, and self-congratulation.

**Fail**

- Says or implies the author is uniquely qualified unless the evidence is clear.
- Overloads rank, degrees, projects, and business history in a way that sounds insecure.

### 5. Voice and brand coherence

**Pass**

- The bio matches the author's literary lane.
- The author's body of work feels coherent.
- Related projects are used only when they strengthen brand clarity.

**Fail**

- The bio reads like a generic LinkedIn summary.
- Project list creates confusion instead of authority.

### 6. Submission appropriateness

**Pass**

- Query-letter bio is short and direct.
- Standalone bio may include more context.
- Website bio can be more atmospheric and brand-forward.

**Fail**

- Same bio is used for every context.
- Query letter includes too much background.
- Website bio is too thin or mechanical.

## Score guide

| Score | Meaning |
|---:|---|
| 9-10 | DREAM/GOLD, submission-ready |
| 8 | Strong, needs light polish |
| 7 | Usable but not premium |
| 6 | Basic but generic or overlong |
| <=5 | Not agent-ready |

## Automatic failures

- Invented author credential.
- Unsupported award/publication/platform claim.
- Placeholder text.
- Unsupported personal identity claim.
- Unsupported residence or regional-experience claim.
- Query bio longer than the query can reasonably support.
- Bio does not connect to the manuscript or author brand at all.

## Preferred RevisionGrade rule

Prefer manuscript-relevant authority over generic literary credentials.

For example, for a Sinaloa-set literary suspense novel, lived regional experience and systems-level professional authority may matter more than generic writing aspirations.
