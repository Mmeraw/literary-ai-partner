DOMINATUS I:4 — Thirst for Change

Earth scrubbed Aqua World, too—one poisoned rain at a time. The surface writhed with putrescence, slick and murky, reflecting only sickness. Below, frogs drifted—sluggish, blistered, blind. The weak floated belly-up, legs splayed, some still twitching as if resisting a verdict already passed. Once, Gorf had cradled these waters, threading life between every ripple. Now, they stank of oil, dishwater, and broken refuse, slapping the shore with mockery. Hyla, Crown of the Columbian spotted frogs, internalized every fading rhythm, feeling each dim against her skin. She waded forward. The sludge swallowed her toes, viscous and unclean. The foulness clung to her like a second skin—sticky, suffocating, sealing her in the knowing. This was no sickness. It was a war declaration. Jagged rocks clawed at the sky like broken teeth. Once, sacred lakes and marshes had shimmered—dreamsicle orange for fleeting beauty, shamrock green for spring’s rebirth, violet for sacred balance. Now, the colors lay buried beneath black muck, as if mourning themselves. Hyla stepped onto hardened shoreline where reeds once hummed with life. The giants' thunderbeasts had flattened everything, leaving the land drenched in slick rainbows: red, silver, blue. Names she could not read but still feared. Ford. Toyota. Chevrolet. Gorf, why do you taunt us so? But she dared not say it aloud. The toadstone stirred beneath her ribs. Not a new voice—but the old one that cradled their Nation. “Rise up. Rise up. Rise all together.” She had sung it as a tadpole. Led it as Crown. Now it pulsed through her like judgment. The Nation’s Song was not a comfort. It was a command. Hyla braced herself. No more waiting. No more silence. Only the long, necessary howl of survival: the time had come to act. The largest cave deep within Aqua World lay in dimness, its shale walls scarred by time, etched with the markings of generations past. Hyla could sense them—the rulers before her, the lost, the ones who had left their wisdom behind. Their pathetic pleas clung to the stone, soaking deep into its marrow. Countless generations of ineptitude now her burden. She perched atop the worn rock at the chamber’s heart. To sit there was to carry the weight of history. A frog had to survive nine ice ages, elevate the colony by acts of wisdom or sacrifice, and possess a generous spirit just to be considered for eldership. Yet even then, it was the Crown that made the final appointment to the Columbian spotted frog council. Hyla outlasted even the hardiest. She owed her longevity to the toadstone. It was not the dark mottling on her skin that betrayed her years, but the weight in her bones—each thickened ring a testament to an ice age spent in hibernation. She lifted her right forearm, twisting it gently, marveling at the sinew stretched over solid bone. Her words carried the weight of generations. “Our ancestors witnessed countless seasons. They adapted, survived, thrived. Yet our fertility rates no longer replenish our losses. It is only a matter of time before—” A bloom of pain flared deep in her stomach—like the fossil was no longer waking but feeding, siphoning truth from her marrow. She winced. A flicker of light at the cave’s entrance drew her notice. Sky Gorf, are you winking me on? Bear down: inhale. Hold. Exhale. Blink. Repeat. Beyond her closed lids, the giants had multiplied—and with them, her prayers. The longer she meditated, the clearer the truth. Aqua World was dying, and no one noticed until the silence grew teeth. Extinction awaits unless we interbreed with a terra firma species. We must traverse land without suffocating. Was that her thought? Or Gorf’s? No. It hummed in her chest, steady as the toadstone’s thrum. This was not a mere whisper. It was truth. A command. The Almighty remained silent. “Crown Hyla—?” “Just a passing discomfort,” she said softly, smoothing her belly with practiced ease. “My body reminds me that time grows short. We face a challenge unlike any before. Our future demands courage and action, not caution.” Wordless contemplation pooled. “Change is daunting,” Hyla continued, “but stagnation is perilous. May we reflect with open minds and courageous hearts, for Aqua World’s fate depends on our unity and resolve.” The silence was not resistance. It was calculation. Who would rise with her? Who would whisper against her once the shadows deepened? Phyto crouched low, pressing her toepads into the moist grains, smoothing them into a rounded mound. “Nature adapts. We must. Resist, and we perish.” Her fingers moved with care—gentle, deliberate. She carved delicate ridges into the sand, shaping a base that felt almost ceremonial. Molly watched—admiring the smooth vitality of Phyto’s skin, supple and unmarred. The Matron, her thin frame shifting and parched skin cracking, joined her, scooping up loose sand and packing it into the structure with careful strokes. “Survival means strengthening what is already here—building on it.” Her delivery was measured, steady. Mage exhaled sharply. “And when that foundation inevitably crumbles?” With a single, decisive stroke of her foot, she flattened the structure. “What is built on weakness always falls.” A stunned pause. Molly recoiled. Phyto’s expression remained unreadable. Hyla’s rib cage caved; she struggled to draw breath. It was always the same. Build. Destroy. Resist. Comply. The debate had just begun. Hyla gathered what little air she could. She would not suffocate in despair. Columbians had ruled, fought, healed. Tonight, the council would decide Aqua World’s fate. She began low, deliberate. “Some things will always remain. The giants wield mystical supa-powers, and it seems no boundary restrains them. They climb trees, leap vast distances, swim, dive, and ride upon hollowed-out, pointy logs, propelling themselves forward with polished sticks. They show no regard for other life—killing insects, felling squirrels, trapping rabbits, and laying low the great beasts: the deer and the bear.” She lowered her forelimbs, toepads sinking into the silty cave floor. The water lapped at her belly, cool and anchoring. Frogs could not clutch—not like the giants. But she could press. Steady. Careful. She coaxed a small mound of sand between her webbed digits. Some granules slipped free, drifting back into the pool below. The rest held—barely. The frogs tensed, then sprang together in a perfect burst. All but Enoch, who had been briefed on the subject of debate. She trusted him to lead the charge. Each elder bore their histories on their skin—thick bones and mottled patterns marking hibernations and rebirths. “The giants command forbidden rites—objects that breathe smoke, carve stone, bend water,” Hyla murmured sharply. “No boundary restrains them. They kill without remorse—flattening beasts, crushing life.” “The only ones who fight back,” she continued,”are the silver-scaled serpents of the abyss…” “I am awed by their struggle—how they thrash to free themselves when ensnared by a giant’s thin, sinister line.” Enoch, the Defender—the only male elder—shifted his weight, his movements more slug-like than frog-like. Ignoring warnings about his health, he often declared, “I live to eat, not the other way around.” His belly settled into the mud. With slow precision, he pressed a white stone into the silt with one forelimb, then braced a black stone against it using the other. His toepads held just enough strain to lift the darker one to chin level. Then—a deliberate drop. The two stones clicked together—not a smash, but a sharp, ceremonial crack that rang out over the water. Gloss above. Rot below. How long before this world shattered? His gaze swept across the others. “Crown Hyla, we can manage unilateral threats—barricades to keep the silver-scales out, hiding from winged terrors, even snakes—but the giants approach from all directions. And when it is a matter of life and death, we must—fight!” Hyla loosened her hold, letting the sand slip away. “Not true, Enoch! How do we resist the poison spewed by the giants’ machines—the water churners bound to their logs? We Columbians evade their venom, but it still drifts toward our homes, seeps into our fortresses, burrows into our skin, corrupting us from within. We are a water-locked species. We do not breathe air the way the giants do. We do not wander from pond to pond without consequence. Our lungs dry. Our skin cracks. Our lifeblood seeps away. You know we cannot simply flee.” The topic gnawed at her. Then— A relentless itching. Something there. Burrowed. Festering. She swam to a jagged stone and scraped against it. Hard. From the grunts, squeals—and screams—Enoch recognized she had reached her limit. The exposed raw tissue confirmed it. As did the bleeding. Exhausted, she fell back, a gloat settling over her. “I crushed those parasites.” What she did not say: She preferred self-inflicted pain over pain delivered by others. Relieved, the Columbian royal continued. “Where was I?” A sharp whistle from Phyto, the Healer. “Ahh, yes. The rainbow slick on Water Gorf’s face, blinding Her—and us. Do you think She suffers the same searing itch we do?” She gestured toward her wounds. “And then we must contend with burns, blisters. Asphyxia. Death. Sometimes the razing is so severe that none of our eggs metamorphose. Insects tainted by the multicolored venom sicken us. How can we protect ourselves?” Hyla squared her shoulders, turned her back to the defender, and forced down the sting of tears. She swam to the cave entrance, spun around, and barked: “We must not be defeatist. Our nation depends upon us to get this right. And I believe the Almighty may finally be on our side.” “She spoke to me.” A pause. A reckoning. “She wants us to explore the notion of—interbreeding.” A hush settled over the chamber. Hyla turned to Phyto. “Phyto, you are closest to Nature. What say you?” “Ah—Ah—Froglore warns that interbreeding leads to male sterility. Females lay half as many eggs—if they lay any at all,” Phyto said. When Phyto spoke, the frogs stilled. Listening was not optional. It was instinct. Not for her voice—but for the shimmer of her skin. Not for what she said, but the surface that said nothing. Smooth. Unblemished. Silent as polished stone. Yet despite her wisdom, most ignored her advice—choosing sweet plants over bitter, protein-rich insects. They stuffed themselves with fiber, pretending not to notice the ache of slow digestion, the creeping bloat of blocked guts. Wanting greater defenses, Enoch rumbled, “Would it not grant us strength? Improved toxins. Imagine being free from water reliance. More choices. Better endurance.” Mage, the Learned—sharp-eyed and critical—countered swiftly. “We are purebloods, descendants of the first terra firma movers. Dilution means loss. And what would stop hybrids from destroying us? Our allegiance binds us together—it does not extend to outsiders.” “It never will,” said Magus—Gorf’s Frog in Aqua World, serene and silver-eyed, her tone calm and certain. “Allow Gorf to decide.” She was the sole inhabitant with silver irises—reflections of the heavier-than-water quicksilver flowing south of their home. That substance had magic, so Magus must as well. Her eyes sat unusually close together, earning her the nickname “narrow-minded,” a title that fit given her endless repetitions of Gorf this, Gorf that. Many whispered that beauty, not merit, had secured her council seat. But Magus knew better. Her lineage was impeccable. Her faith unshakable. Let their whispers fall silent—her dedication spoke louder than their doubts. She had once fasted through the eclipse, eyes glazed, chanting beneath the quicksilver flow. She purged and emptied her body to make room for the visions. And the mercury-voice demanded a deeper hollow or the Realm’s darkest whisper could not fill her. The mercury had touched her tongue—staining it black. A mark of proximity. A price. She had waited for the thrum to align: eclipse, stone, and sky. Not for beauty. For revelation. She did not speak for shimmer. She spoke for what lay beyond it—where breath burns and the Realm listens. “To Mage’s point,” Arcana interjected, “it would be more than physical dilution—it would be cultural loss.” She lifted her forelimbs, scattering invisible seeds. “And disease. What if foreign illnesses wipe us out? Perhaps we should test this far from our home.” Hyla interjected, stone-faced. “Cultural appropriation?” Mage fidgeted, pressing her toepads together—like she was testing the weight of her own convictions. “Your Highness, if we breed with another species, the first generation will be half pure. The next, half again, until we disappear entirely. Trust would erode. Diversity would fracture our society. Inter-tribal warfare exists everywhere—why risk it?” Enoch said, “Denser populations mean less food, less privacy, and more disease. Trust erodes. I tend to agree.” Arcana said, “How can you be certain our line has been pure since the beginning? Maybe our words remained constant, but our bodies evolved.” Mage countered, voice firm. “Then our strength is not in flesh, but in the stories that endure.” She did not blink when she said it. Her ancestors’ migration had been reduced to myth, their long trek between ponds no longer verifiable. Still, she clung to it. If the stories failed, what else would remain? Arcana leaned in. “But stories shift—how do we know a past ruler did not alter our truths? The Divine Will of Gorf demands blind obedience to the toadstone’s keeper. Who is to say the words were never changed?” She reached down and drew a single line in the sand, her toepad pressing firmly into the damp grains. “This is our past.” Her finger drifted to the right. “We have traveled this far, and this is where we stand now.” Then, slowly, she extended the line forward, dragging it farther across the earth. “If I extend it, that is our future.” She met their gazes. “But how do we know we are drawing it in the right direction?” She knew the weight of that question. Her sister had once drawn too far—into hybrid waters—and never returned. No body. No name spoken. Just erasure. That silence did not teach obedience. It taught suspicion. Arcana questioned everything—especially Gorf’s scripture. Enoch stretched, yawned, then eased into place. “Arcana, life is more than lines in the sand.” He let the words settle. “There is a bigger struggle here: how do we ensure mixing does not compromise our values and beliefs? Whether it would be right for us or not, there are arguments on both sides. There is no definitive answer—only conjecture. Regardless of our path, we must be cautious about controlling population growth. That means preserving and protecting our kind and the spaces we inhabit.” He coughed—wet, deep. It had grown worse each season. The spores in his chest were likely blooming already. But he would speak until they silenced him. “We could debate this forever, but it would be fruitless. There is only one certainty: Gorf gave us life, and we must trust Her plans for us.” Mage glanced at Hyla. “If the Supreme Being has spoken through the toadstone—if interbreeding is our future—who are we to deny it?” Silence settled, demanding the final word. But Death moved through it, guiding Silence, Darkness, and Dampness to shadow the frogs. I:4.1 Then—a crack. Something broke. A split in the rock-fortification barrier. Silver-scales surged through—dead-eyed, vicious, unstoppable. Sleek bodies. A flash. A slice through water. A tremor rolled through the depths. A scrape of scales against stone. The stink of fish—sharp, briny, suffocating—soured the water. Loose scales drifted past, weightless and wrong, torn free in the frenzy. Razor-sharp fins slashing. Frogs panicked—limbs flailing. Some scrambled. Others twisted helplessly in snapping jaws. The water churned crimson. Enoch lunged into the fray. “Sentinels! Scouts! Reinforce the walls!” Too late. The breach widened. Hyla froze, her eyes bulging, limbs locking. This was no ordinary attack. It was execution. It was judgment. It was a reckoning. “Enoch!” she barked. “Close the gap—now!” But the damage was irreversible. Blood swirled thickly, mixing with poison, staining their home forever. The wind would carry the dead. Blood’s vanquished assault clung to their nostrils—acrid, suffocating. Hyla took in the crimson waters, her vent tightening. Silver-scales were merciless. But a greater threat loomed. Fresh giant tracks. Too large. Too many. Impossible to ignore. She touched her fingers to the hum in her chest—a warped vibration. Not comfort. Not grounding. Just there. Her skin prickled. The attack had proven one thing—existence demanded adaptation. Mage was shaking. Her limbs crossed over her soft belly, as if shielding the place where breath and memory collided. Her gullet fluttered with uneven gulps. She had seen it before. She had known it before. But this was different. She had been close enough to hear bones splinter. To see a frog’s belly slit clean in a single slice of a razor fin. Her fingers clenched, squeezing mucus from her skin. “You saw what happened,” she rasped. “You still think mixing with outsiders is the answer?” Enoch’s torso tightened. “I do,” he growled. “If we were stronger, we could fight back.” Arcana went still. Too still. Her toepads rested lightly on her belly, her wide eyes fixed on an object—no, someone—floating between the stalks. A leg. Torn. Detached. Stripped of muscle, swaying gently in the current. The voice that debated her this morning. Gone. Magus smoothed a lily flower, her movements measured—a ritual to keep her chest from shaking. “Call it frog, toad, spawnling, abomination—it bleeds the same. Unity is in the rupture, not the name.” Molly rose slowly, her nose wrinkling against the stench of death, eyes squinting as though the sunlight itself offended her. With the stiffness of age, she moved to stimulate the flower. Though barren, she commanded reverence—not through fertility, but through duty. “Magus is correct. Regardless of what hatches, we are bound to care for them,” she said. The water shuddered with unease. No warmth. No sentiment. Only duty. For Molly, motherhood was not a choice. It was law. Enoch edged forward, then rose to full height. He stretched out his forelimbs, drawing the trembling females into his warm, steady center. “Steady yourselves,” he murmured, firm and low. “We must be strong. The colony expects us to lead.” The weight of his certainty eased their quaking limbs. Her sides no longer shuddered; she stood unwavering. “The soul is what travels. Life here is a test of worthiness.” Phyto’s eye ridges lifted, moist skin twitching with restrained energy. “If a giant or snake caught you, would you not wish for a stinger? Or stronger poison? Now, we face unprecedented threats. We must explore paths untraveled.” Hyla’s vent fluttered, her skin buzzing with unease. “Doing nothing ensures extinction.” The frogs stayed silent. Mouths tight. But their eyes—those that dared meet hers—were raw, glistening. “I go alone,” Hyla declared. Her words cut through the trembling air—clean, sharp, sovereign. A few frogs flinched. One gasped. Mage recoiled. Arcana choked, like another limb had just gone under. Enoch waited. No protest. No hesitation. He shifted his weight, grounding himself. He had made his decision. “No one follows.” A pause. Arcana eked out a final, desperate plea. “And if you do not return?” Hyla searched the polluted water. “Then allow Gorf to speak.” Stillness. She pivoted, toepads striking the water’s surface, ripples fanning outward. May they doubt. May they tremble. She had already decided. Then— A pulse. Mount Meager spat its fury into the sky not far from here—just eighty kilometers east, a mere two and a half millennia ago. The spores had slumbered beneath that soot, nestled in ash-thick pools.
