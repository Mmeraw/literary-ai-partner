Chapter 2 – What the River Already Knew

Location: Sáh Degh Village → Smokehouse Camp, NWT
July 13, 2025 (one day earlier)

The river didn’t just pull forward; it judged backward—already weighing us, deciding what to keep. We hadn’t met William yet—it had.

***

A day before NV115 reappeared.
Before the silence at Stonepine Flats.

***

We were still heading south from the Arctic, sticky with bug spray and trail sweat gone sour. You stop noticing the residue until a breeze reminds you you’ve worn the same shirt for four days. Eleven days of repellent, grime, and hand sanitizer had formed a second skin. We stopped only for gas and a windshield wipe—not for people or prophecy. The road ahead unwound in a heat-blurred ribbon. Dogs crashed in their crate in the back, claws twitching in sleep.
About an hour south of Fort Simpson, we pulled into a tidy, two-pump gas station with a log-cabin-style convenience store in Sáh Degh Village. It was the kind of place where you could imagine winter snow banked up to the window ledges. But today, the pavement wavered with heat. On the patio, a coffee table perched above the pumps and the gravel road beyond.
Inside, it smelled faintly of pine cleaner and cardboard. French-vanilla lattes from a self-serve machine—$3.15 each. Summer windshield fluid: four liters at $14.00. Cliff hesitated at the price, just like he did in Prince George, where it was $7.99. We needed it, so I bought it. Both pumps were out of service. The next town had gas, they said. We’d see.
After topping off the washer fluid, we let the dogs out, fed them the last foam from our cups, and walked them in the patch of grass by the side lot. Their paws kicked up the dry earth, tails high, happy to stretch. That’s when she appeared.
Not mystical. Just present. A woman in her thirties: short black hair swept up, dark sunglasses hiding her gaze. Tank top with a halter, arms freckled and sun-warmed. Chipped pink polish on exposed toes. No rings. Bare nails. Solid as a hillside—broad, steady, nothing to prove. Stillness that spoke for itself.
We hadn’t planned to linger—for fish, for people, for anything but gas. Beards itched against bug bites. Backs stiffened from trailer sleep and the tight arc of daily driving. As we walked the dogs, the woman approached us, her steps purposeful.
“Hello. You guys interested in Tłekeh Dene First Nation hospitality? We’re serving fish at Smokehouse Camp, down by the Upper Wathah River.”
I handed Cliff the dogs and backed off—no interest in small talk with a stranger. Cliff, being Cliff, either didn’t notice or didn’t care. Within a minute, he was hers. Food was the collar. She held the leash.
“Heh, Mike. You wanna eat fish? Down by the river. No cooking. Save us time. Free.”
On cue, the woman joined in. “Just up the road toward Smokehouse Camp. Follow the signs. They’ll take you left down to the water. All are welcome. Lotta fish came in. Too much for us. We like to share.”
Cliff shrugged. “What do we got to lose? In and out in an hour. And did I say free?”
She gave a bright smile, but not an invested one. Like the answer didn’t matter. 
“We’ve got cake too.”
Then she turned and walked off, sandals slapping pavement. Halfway to the store, she paused, turned back.
“William’s there,” she said. “He’s ninety-two. Likes meeting new folks. He dreamed of you last night.”
No pressure. No mystery. Just an offer. But it stuck.
Once she was out of earshot, Cliff said, “She seems nice enough. Trustworthy. Think it’s a scam?”
“Why would anyone lure strangers to a Smokehouse Camp? Hell of a scam, don’t you think? It’s not a timeshare pitch.”
“Well, I’m hungry. All they can do is kick us out if we aren’t welcome.”
As we pulled out of the lot, her voice stayed with me. Maybe it was how she mentioned William—like he was a neighbor, not bait. Maybe it was the cake. Or the pause, like she knew we’d come.

***

Less than ten minutes later, we’d passed the edge of the village and were descending a steep gravel road toward the river. Two large, hand-painted fish-shaped signs pointed the way. The road curved through shrubs and birch, the trees thinning at the bottom where the forest opened to the sky.
The camp opened ahead—seven permanent shelters, each about ten by fifteen feet, blue tarps lashed tight over beam-framed roofs. Four were plain service huts—storage, hygiene, a place to sleep—sided in wood but patched with plywood where needed. Three were smokehouses. Off to the side stood an empty white tarpaulin tent. A pair of hand-lettered wooden boards leaned against one wall:

SMOKEHOUSE CAMP
SHARE WHAT YOU CAN

Every child is special.
Nurture and protect them.

No tourist signs. No plaques. Just thirty or so people doing the work of feeding their families. Three pickup trucks. A scatter of ATVs.
We slowed, eased the Jeep to a quiet stop, a good distance from the camp. Far enough that our engine and dogs wouldn’t intrude. The dogs howled, noses already alive with the scent of fire, bark mulch, water, fish.
Cliff killed the engine.
I stepped out first. Didn’t head for the wood houses. Didn’t reach for food. Just stood, scanning the grounds.
The forest and grounds were better groomed than Cliff’s property in Bralorne.
Wood chips adorned the area. They helped keep the dust and sand down during windstorms. Wood was stacked up in front of the forest in uniform piles. I’d later learn that forest management was in full force. The wood was a coveted asset for building, burning, and grinding.
The first person I saw was a lean Indigenous guy, mid-twenties, fidgeting, pacing. A wooden smokehouse stood to my right; he moved in and out of view like a shadow skipping across its doorway. His jeans sagged below the waist, the underwear band showing. He kept hitching them up, muttering—not clear if to himself or to someone out of sight. Cream-colored fleece clung to his frame, with sleeves that were too long. Hair close-cropped. His most noticeable feature—large diamond pendants in each ear—caught and scattered the sun.
From somewhere beyond him, I heard a holler—a voice shaped in authority, wrapped in an RCMP uniform, drawing closer.
That’s when I saw him.
William.
A thin old man in a camp chair at the river’s edge, back to the water, observing everything. Black ball cap with a faded logo, long sleeves in the heat, mauve flannel under a dark windbreaker. A checkered wool blanket—blue, gray, and white—draped over his knees. His hands, spotted and arthritic, rested steady. A polished walking stick between his legs. Jeans loose over clean black sneakers—pristine, like newly unboxed.
I walked toward him. “You must be William.”
His voice came clear and steady—age had curled his fingers and slowed his step, but hadn’t dulled his tone. “Gloria found you.”
“Yes.”
He tipped his head toward a woman in her mid-thirties with long black hair, seated beside him. “This is Brie. My grandniece. She hovers more than she should, always certain I will break if left too long on my own.”
We nodded. No handshakes. It didn’t feel wrong.
“She is the one who keeps me honest about the day-to-day. Her sister, Amber—my other grandniece—is the record keeper. Water clarity, fish runs, algae blooms. If something shifts, she writes it down. But she is not here today.”
Brie began to stand; I motioned for her to stay seated, and she remained in her chair.
“Back in ’49,” William said, “we trapped with sled dogs. Before the road. Before white diesel. Before strangers like you.”
Cliff let out a thin laugh, unsure if it was humor or a warning. “And before the river started talking.”
He lifted his chin toward the dark shimmer beyond the clearing. The river moved slowly, the surface like oiled glass.
“It talks,” he said. “Sometimes. When something wakes it.”
Brie leaned in, her attention as fixed as mine. William didn’t speak again right away. It was as if he was listening to something just beneath the rest of our noise—the shuffle of boots on gravel, the soft hiss of fire on damp wood, the occasional metallic clink from a knife meeting bone.
I filled the pause. “Yes, William, Gloria at the gas station invited us. We’re glad she did. We just finished visiting the Arctic Ocean and are heading back south. Every time we speak to locals, they point us to hidden jewels.” I spread my arms and slowly turned a circle. “And this is clearly one. After eleven days of only dogs, you might be stuck with Cliff and me as your new best friends.”
Brie smiled; William’s mouth tugged at one corner.
“And I’m Mike. My step-dad, Cliff, you’ve already met.”
Brie excused herself, leaving us with William and his stories. He spoke of trapping in the 1940s and 1950s. He described walking frozen rivers in moosehide boots and crude shelters through long winters. The cadence of his voice was like the river—measured, deliberate, not in a rush to empty out. He carried us to the Dew Line and back within minutes, threading his sentences from one decade to another like they belonged to the same afternoon.
He didn’t complain. He remembered.
Cliff and I remained standing, leaning in to listen closely to William.
William’s cap shaded his eyes; his lips barely moved. I had to read them as much as listen. 
When he paused, I said, “Quiet out here.”
“One time, it was louder,” he said. “Birds everywhere.” His voice was gravel-soft—worn but not weak.
Then the RCMP constable arrived—tall, wide, pale as drift ice. Flame-red ponytail down her back, a signal fire against muted camp tones. Vest bulked her chest, belt heavy with gun, baton, flashlight, cuffs. No taser. Her boots whispered through the mulch, soft but undeniable in their intent.
She wasn’t here to check in. She was here to remind someone who owns the dirt under their boots.
Her target was the pacing young man. He drifted toward the picnic table beside William with a plate of food wrapped in foil.
“You’ve been here four hours already,” she said. “Time to leave.”
He rose—slowly, not aggressively—the disappointment in his movement louder than words. He backed toward the river without turning away. He pulled out a cell phone, then angled toward the parking area. He muttered a few words and came back. 
“I didn’t finish my fish.”
Hands on her belt, she said nothing and motioned for him to leave. 
When he moved toward her and reached out to touch her shoulder, she spun backward, hand closing over the gun’s grip.
“No!”
The shout cut across the camp—louder than the water.
He backed toward the river—eyes on her, committing her face to memory.
Cliff whispered, “Colonialism takes many forms. Still happening—again and again.”
The camp’s rhythm faltered. Knives slowed. Conversations thinned. Even the smoke from the curing sheds thinned.
The young man retreated toward the bank, phone back in hand, as an ATV arrived, with an Indigenous woman at the handlebars. He left his plate of food with a thin smile, turned his back to the constable, walked to the ATV, and hopped on. The couple roared up the hill in a burst of exhaust and dust.
“Red” walked off without acknowledging us. Minutes later, the truck that brought her in whisked her away, swallowed by the road to Fort Simpson.
No one spoke for or against the man. It was like someone had cut in a reel from another film and spliced it into the day.
Eventually, I drifted toward the nearest wooden shelter and peeked inside. A middle-aged woman with long black hair stood at a washstand, her hands moving in a deep basin of water. Behind her, a heavyset man in his fifties sat, warmth creeping across his face, wearing a gray cotton pullover and jeans, as if the comfort of the place belonged to him.
No electricity—just filtered daylight spilling through slatted rafters and blue tarp, softening the curl of boneless white fish flesh drying above us like petals. The air smelled of wet wood, salt, and faint, clean smoke. No screens. No flies. Just quiet shadows and meat. 
“Hi, I’m Mike. William said I could take a look around. Need help with dishes? Toilets, floors—I’m not picky.”
It was one of my standard lines.
The woman chuckled softly. “Yes, you can. But only after you’ve eaten.”
The man nodded, his face unchanged since I’d walked in, the same quiet presence lingering. 
“Okay, I’ll take you up on your offer,” I said, stepping back toward the heart of the camp—where the most people were gathered.
Two women and two boys worked the waist-high block. Large fish lay there—whole, headless, split. Hands in gloves moved in rhythm, skinning fillets, lifting bones clean.
At the head stood an elder, maybe seventy-five, short blade in one hand, gut bucket at her feet. Thin as a reed, hands wired with sinew. She was beheading the fish and stripping out the guts, throwing them into two five-gallon blue buckets on the ground to one side of the table.
“You see this?” she said, nodding toward the heap of flesh and offal. “Most days, we’re lucky to pull two fish an hour. Today? Six came in at once. Feels like the river decided to be generous.”
To one side, two boys worked shoulder to shoulder—one wiry and quick, the other careful, tongue poked out in concentration, deboning fish, then slicing the meat into thin strips—like beef jerky before it’s dried.
To the other side, two five-gallon blue buckets of fish heads and guts.
I introduced myself to those around the table. “You eat all that?” I asked, nodding toward the buckets brimming with fish heads, livers, and pale, folded stomach lining.
The elder looked up. “I’m Nila. ’Course. We eat everything—except the bones.”
Her smile was crooked, with the top teeth gone, but the bottom row was white and straight, like she was halfway through getting a full set of implants. A smile with more grip than most handshakes.
She lifted a slick piece of gut and dropped it into the bucket. It landed with a wet slap.
“You eat those too?”
She shrugged. “Special dish: tsʼoh tłʼit.” She checked whether I’d flinch at the Tłekeh Dene name. “Not for everyone. But it’s good—if you stop thinking about what it is. Check it out later. Last pan on the table. Only black one, near the bannock.”
I watched the boys. The quick one moved like this was his hundredth fish. The deliberate one moved like the fish was still alive—on life support—and he didn’t want to kill it.
“Hello, Mike. I’m Dana. That one’s Jackson,” said a woman behind me.
I turned. She was in her late thirties, filming and angling for the moment the blade cleared the spine.
“He’s new. First day with a blade. Trying hard. That one—” she nodded toward his companion—”that’s Teddy. Been at it a year. Moves like his uncle.”
Two younger children, both smaller —a boy and a girl —were clinging to their mother as they watched nearby.
Proud without boasting, mothers hovered—teaching without touching. Phones propped up at the fringes—cracked screens, a tripod leaning in the dirt.
That’s when Teddy dropped a strip of skin, long and curled like a ribbon. He held it under his nose like a mustache, eyes crossed, making the others howl. “Look, I’m Grandpa!” he squeaked, wobbling the fish mustache until it slapped his chin. The women shushed him, but they were smiling too. Ripples of snickers and whispers ran through the line, the scrape of knives against boards, then more laughter when Jackson tossed a skin strip at Teddy and it stuck to his sleeve. For a moment, the camp was children’s noise—bright, high, alive—threaded through the steady rhythm of blades and smoke.
The woman in front of Nila dropped a fish in the dirt, picked it up, and brushed it clean—careful, deliberate, like pulling splinters. The ground clung to its skin, grit and wood chips working into the scales. With no running water at Smokehouse Camp, hands did the work—gloves, napkins, whatever could be spared—until the fish was fit for the pan again.
“Have you been here a while?” I said.
“We have,” the woman said. “Preparing fish, can’t wait.”
“What kind of fish?”
“Coney and Whitefish. Dense meat. Flavorful. Coney’s what we call it here—some say White Salmon.”
“How big do they get?”
“Up to three feet, twenty-five pounds. One fish can feed a family for a week.”
Smoke drifted low and lay spice across the rafters.
“Dana, is that your boy?” I asked, nodding toward Jackson.
“Yeah. First day cutting. He’s proud. Trying not to show it.”
“He’s doing well.”
Her mouth lifted. “He’ll get faster.”
“Can I take a peek inside the smokehouses? Maybe a photo?”
“Sure.”
Inside, the light thinned to amber. Elongated counters ran along two walls. On them: fish in every stage of drying. On the dirt floor, heaps of guts and scraps were dumped from dishpans onto polyethylene sheeting.
At the far end, four logs smoldered—not enough to choke, just enough to cure. My lungs were grateful; light asthma made me sensitive to smoke.
Beams overhead—stripped logs—held rows of fish strips. A few whole fish dangled by their tails, twine blackened from use. Maybe remnants of two hundred fish in all.
Through the slatted rafters and tarp roof, soft daylight sifted in. There were no insect barriers at the open ends—though oddly, no bugs in sight. Still, the notion of flies laying eggs in the meat lodged in the back of my mind. I kept it there.
When I stepped back out, I spotted a man on the tailgate of an older black Dodge crew cab. Early forties, lean.
“Name’s Mike. Looks like you’ve got the easiest job here. Supervisor?” I opened my eyes wide, studying the expanse. 
“I’m Martin. Supervisor? Of these women? Not a chance. They’re self-sufficient. We just get in the way.”
“But you maintain the camp?”
“Yeah. Forest, grounds, buildings, vehicles.”
“Impressive,” I said. “One of those kids yours?”
“Yeah—Jackson.”
“The one in the green shirt?”
“Yup.”
I turned to the woman beside me—Dana. “I thought he was your husband.”
“How’d you know?”
“The way he keeps checking you out.” I glanced at her backside for emphasis.
Martin and the others cracked up.
“You got any single women here looking for trouble?” I asked.
Nila, the elder, didn’t miss a beat. She pointed to a woman sitting near a white tarpaulin tent. “Yes. Tajina. Right there.”
Tajina looked up, caught her name, dropped her gaze, and spooned food into her mouth.
I took two quick steps toward her. The women behind me broke into laughter.
Dana piped up, “Actually, we’ve got two!” Another woman—alone—was walking toward the cutting block. “Mike, come meet Robin.”
Robin stopped, jerked her head back, and turned away.
I shot Nila and Dana a look. “Two at once? That’d finish me.”
Nila grinned. “And more.”
Dana just nodded.
Laughter swelled, quick as fire over dry bark—then broke, leaving me aware of the ratio of women to men. Maybe three to one. Where were the men? Off working? Gone for good?
And if they were gone, what did life look like for a woman who stayed?
By then, Cliff had wandered over. I introduced him. Martin lit a cigarette.
“No good for you, you know,” Cliff said.
“I’ll quit eventually. Got time.”
“You might be right. I quit fifty years ago, and I’m still standing.”
I jumped in. “Hey, Martin, do you hunt like William did?”
“Not exactly. We’ve got ATVs, trucks, motorboats now. What’s still the same is the rhythm—we hunt year-round. Elk, caribou, moose, duck, grouse, rabbit. But the numbers? Falling. Every year.”
“What about vegetables? Fruit?”
“Not much. Berries sometimes. A little lettuce. Maybe some potatoes or hardy cabbage from the school garden now and then.”
Nila cut in. “Don’t be shy. Try the fish. Guts are in the black pan at the end. Take a spoonful.”
Laughter broke loose—even Jackson, slicing fish carefully as a surgeon, cracked a grin.
Cliff had already eaten, so I headed for the center of camp.
Three long tables stretched end to end, white vinyl cloths printed with sunflowers. Two dozen aluminum pans filled with fish lined their tops. Baked, broiled, salted, smoked, dried. Two steaming pots of soup.
Sturdy steel chairs with vinyl backs ringed the setup—worn but permanent. No flimsy folding seats here; nothing temporary about how this community gathered to eat.
Off to the side, three makeshift stoves stood ready: halved forty-five-gallon drums, flipped open, filled with glowing charcoal. Grills rested on top, soup and water pots steaming above them.
First thing I tried? The guts.
Raw, I’d seen them—slick loops and pale folds. Cooked, they seemed almost innocent. In a cast-iron skillet, the mash resembled scrambled eggs gone soft. A purple spatula rested inside. Not a drop of blood in sight.
Just a gray-beige mash, thick as wet oatmeal, hiding its secrets.
Two spoonfuls later, I’d done it. Downed the infamous “black pan” dish.
I filled a paper bowl with more food and walked back toward Cliff and Martin.
That’s when a loud white pickup rolled in—long trailer, ATV on top. Alberta plates.
The driver stepped out: mirrored sunglasses, cargo shorts, sleeveless shirt. Alone.
He walked like he belonged somewhere—just not here.
No nod. No smile. Straight past the smokehouses, past the people, to the food.
Two paper plates piled high, a bowl of soup, disposable cutlery. Then he went back to his truck. Door open. Ate alone in the cab.
When he finished, he tossed everything—plates, bowl—into the truck bed. No bag.
Then cracked a beer, chugged it, threw the can in with the rest.
I muttered, “That’ll bounce out the second he hits gravel.”
Martin watched him pull a jerry can from the trailer, then slop gas into the tank. A wet splash streaked the truck’s side, darkening the grime, hitting the ground. He didn’t bother to wipe it.
“I’ll get that later,” Martin said.
“With a shovel?”
“Yeah. Throw down some bark shred, soak it up, scoop it, burn it.”
“Nature’s sponge,” I said. “I’d have used paper towels and tossed it in a garbage bag.”
“And then the bag ends up in a landfill,” he said, “where the toxins leach into the soil. Into the water table. Into the rivers that feed the lakes and oceans. Some of it settles. Some evaporates, rides the wind into the jet stream, circles the world, settles on everything—manmade and natural.”
I paused. Let it settle. The river behind us moved with a slow, unreadable count.
“When I was in Vladivostok, Russia with Her Majesty’s Canadian Ship (HMCS) Protecteur in ’99,” I said, “I saw concrete walls pitted like bad skin. Acid rain damage.”
Martin nodded. “That’s what we see. Imagine what we don’t. It’s like people buyin’ up parcels of land near the creeks, always in cash, always through numbered companies. The sellers tell us the buyers never show up in person—they send lawyers and sign in absentia.”
Cliff joined in, and soon the talk turned to trucks and differentials. But I kept one eye on the man with Alberta plates.
Smokehouse Camp had been easy in its rhythm. This man was grit in the gears—small, irritating, impossible to ignore.
People watched him without meaning to. Quietly. Repeatedly.
He finished his beer and started his engine. No thanks. No nod. Drove off.
William didn’t flinch. Still seated, still impassive. He’d seen the type before. Probably had.
Then he stopped—attention on the far end of camp.
Anthony.
He came toward us—tall and thin, almost feminine, stretched like wire. Black from head to toe—hat, shirt, long pants, scuffed runners. Lithe like a shadow. Glasses large and round with thick frames, like old binoculars cut in half. He carried the awkwardness of fifteen, the wear of sixty.
In both hands, he carried a small foil tray, as if it were a fragile glass. Inside: three-quarters of a dark cake. Moist, dense.
He didn’t wait for us to speak. Just hovered, the cake steady between his palms.
“For you,” he said. “From William. Give some to the dogs too. It’s a healthy choice.”
His voice was flat, calm.
I stood. “Dogs can’t eat chocolate—especially dark.”
“It’s not chocolate,” Anthony said. “Looks like it. But it’s ground black spruce bark and fireweed honey. For ceremony. Safe for all kin—two- and four-legged.”
“Wow. Looks like chocolate cake. Dark. Spongy. That strawberry topping?”
“Yes. Creamy strawberry-white icing.”
“Killer combo.”
Dana came over. “Only for your waistline. Feed your four-legged ones. They’ve got spirit, same as you.”
“I guess if I asked for the recipe, I’d be hard pressed to find the ingredients and make it, right?”
Dana’s eyes brightened. “It’s traditional. Plant-based. Non-toxic to animals. Made with carob, sweetened with birch syrup and berries. The topping? Fresh strawberries, icing sugar, cream.”
Anthony added, “Let them try it.”
Dana nodded. “We don’t bake poison.”
I put two pieces into a paper bowl already loaded with leftovers.
“Not too much,” Cliff warned. “They’ll puke all over the Jeep.”
Anthony nodded. Remained glued in place like an expectant mother. Didn’t move until we ate—all of it.
It was the only thing we didn’t serve ourselves.
Cliff loved it.
Me? I couldn’t taste much after the first forkful. My tongue went a little numb. Maybe the sugar. Maybe something else.
Someone passed mugs of tea.
Cliff sniffed his. “Fireweed.”
“Tastes like earth and molasses,” I said.
“Reminds me of Grandpa’s stove,” he said after a sip. “Only thing missing is kerosene.”
Anthony was still there, tray lowered to his side, his hands folded like he’d been listening the whole time. His glasses were blanking out his eyes.
“You brought ghosts with you,” he said.
Cliff frowned. “What kind of ghosts?”
Anthony tilted his head toward the river. “The kind that don’t leave. The kind that call back when you step too close.”
He set the tray on his knee, reached into his pocket, and pulled out a folded square of birch bark. Unwrapped it slowly, spreading it flat across his palm. Lines inked by hand. Watersheds. Rivers marked with symbols, Xs pressed into the grain.
“These aren’t the ones taken,” he said. His voice was calm, almost childlike. “They’re the ones spoken for.”
William shifted in his chair but didn’t interrupt. His silence was permission.
Anthony touched the Mackenzie, finger resting where the lines branched wide. “She’s the oldest. She began the remembering.”
Cliff leaned forward, knuckles white on his notebook. “So the rivers choose?”
Anthony shook his head. “Not choice. Balance. You take too much, it answers. Not with anger. With memory.”
He turned to William then, his voice dropping almost to a whisper. “Your brother knew. He saw it first.”
William didn’t shift. But the fire spat once, like it was agreeing.
Anthony stood, lifted the tray—only a sliver of cake left—and slipped toward a wooden house at the camp’s edge.
The texture lingered: dense, grainy, not too sweet. Not the best cake I’d had, but the most seriously delivered.
Had the man with Alberta plates been offered any?

***

We made one last pass through camp. Most legs were dark pants and leggings built for use, cloth that hid dust and matched the ground. It read like a choice, not a uniform. When the group moved together, it became one body—hard to tell where one person ended until a face rose from the shade.
That made the exceptions louder. William in sky-blue jeans. Another man in work-blue, older, stooped. Their denim didn’t just break pattern; it set its own. Years had earned them that right. Nobody said a word; no one needed to.
What the fabric said was simple: we belong to this place, not to store-bought colors from the far south. Kids wore black early; elders had worn it so long that even memory felt dyed. The point wasn’t absence—it was ground. Whispers trailed after them—not hostile, not mocking, but attentive, as if the cloth spoke. 
We went back to William to bid farewell.
“Say goodbye to the river too,” he said. “She fed us. She takes care of us. She will take care of you.”
Cliff and I looked at each other, then back at him.
“Some say she is older than any calendar we ever trusted,” he continued. “If you listen carefully, she speaks. Especially when the fish stop biting. Means she wants something. Rivers need to feed too. She gives dreams. She gives warnings and reminders. And sometimes—she gives back something you lost.”
He paused.
“But she always takes something else in return.”
Lost? During our trip, I’d lost my milk frother. A pair of socks. Inconsequential. 
Shut up, Mike—now’s not the time for humor.
“You ever see a creek eat a man, Cliff? Not swallow—eat. There is a difference.”
And then his story—February, Jean Marie River, ten dogs, weak ice, water silent as it swallowed him to the hip, left boot snagged in brush. The ice lip broke under each grip until the lead dog held her ground and pulled him back into life.
“It knew I was coming. I stepped wrong. It answered.”
The river behind him shimmered dark, as if it remembered.
Cliff and I shared a quick beat, the kind when the ground shifts beneath a conversation. Fritz and Schultz had been his disciples earlier; now we’d taken their place.
Cliff stood and offered his hand. William stayed seated, met him halfway—nearly two centuries of life between them compressed into one handshake. 
How long would it take them to trade their full stories, given the chance?
Anthony was beside William again, so Cliff shook his hand too. Then mine.
William lifted his cap—whether to adjust it or scratch an itch, I couldn’t tell. It was the first time I saw his eyes: deep-set hollows, shadowed, the brown dulled to the color of wet cedar bark. A fringe of white hair circled the back of his head; the crown polished to a shine like Cliff’s.
He was still talking—about trapping lines, cold shelters, waiting for the thaw to come so he could return home—when we turned to leave.

***

At the Jeep, the dogs devoured the fish. But the cake?
They slowed. Ate it gently. Noses twitching like they’d caught a memory instead of a scent.
Then they curled at our feet—ears cocked toward the river, attention on its dark shimmer, as if waiting for something to rise.
I had to lift them back into the crate.
By the time we pulled away, the sun was low. The river caught its light in bronze shards, scattering them downstream like spent offerings.
And we still had no plan for where to sleep.

***

Cliff settled in behind the wheel, his posture easy, like the day had been an old trail he’d walked before.
“Heh, Dad, how many women did you talk to at Smokehouse Camp?”
“None.”
“Why?”
“Because none approached me. You’re the yappy one.” He gave me that half-grin that could be pride or pity. “Besides, I don’t need any more women in my life. I want to die in peace.”
“That’s going on your headstone.”
We both laughed—loud enough that the dogs stirred, shifting in the crate. Fritz yawned, and Schultz tilted his head. 
I’m going to miss you both when I go back to Mexico.
The road rose away from the riverbank, tires biting gravel. Trees closed in, their leaves muting the last of the light.
Somewhere behind us, William’s chair still faced the camp. The river moved slowly and darkly, holding the day’s conversations like driftwood—deciding whether to keep them, return them, or take them for good.
William’s people said some meetings are chosen before you arrive. What did they think ours was for?
